# Addendum II (June 2026) — 10k-seed PRNG audit & the "hourly pattern" thesis

> **Date:** 2026-06-15
> **Sample:** 10,000 consecutive ground-truth rounds (IDs 6691690–6701689,
> market `ng`), spanning ~51.9 h, pulled from the unauthenticated
> `/round/{id}/seeds` endpoint.
> **Integrity:** 10,000 / 10,000 rounds re-derived from seeds match the API
> exactly (0 mismatches).
> **Result:** The 48-bit operator `serverSeed` is indistinguishable from a
> cryptographic RNG, and **the "certain multipliers per hour" thesis is real but
> not exploitable** — it is a clock-time artifact of multiplier-dependent round
> durations, with zero per-round predictability.

This addendum answers a direct challenge to the two prior studies: *"within every
hour, certain multipliers are guaranteed to appear a number of times — surely we
can dig deeper and find an exploitable pattern."* We attacked it on the only two
fronts the prior work left open:

1. **The 48-bit `serverSeed` generator** — never audited at scale (prior glance: n=45).
2. **The timing of multipliers within the hour** — the user's specific claim.

## 1. Data collection & integrity

`scripts/sh_seed_collect.py` backfills consecutive round IDs from the public
seeds endpoint (resumable, rate-limited, 4 polite workers), storing each round's
`serverSeed`, ordered `clientSeeds`, `generatedHash`, `decimal`,
`houseCoefficient`, and `startTime` into SQLite. **Every row is integrity-checked**
by recomputing `SHA-512(serverSeed-cs1-…-csN) → decimal → houseCoefficient` and
asserting it equals the API's value.

- **10,000 / 10,000 verified**, **0 mismatches** → the Bustabit formula is now
  confirmed at n=10⁴ (vs n=45 previously).
- Dataset: `data/sh_seeds_10k.jsonl`.

### Formula correction (resolves the old 10.7% vs 3% dispute)

The minimum *winning* step the display can show is **1.01x**, so a round shows
**1.00x (instant bust)** whenever the raw value rounds below 1.005:

```
houseCoefficient = max(1.00, round(0.97 · 2³² / (2³² − decimal), 2))
```

P(instant bust) = P(raw < 1.005) = 1 − 0.97/1.005 = **3.483%**. Observed: **3.490%**
(349/10000, binomial p = 0.96). The original study's ~10.7% "edge" was an artifact
of the on-screen scraper over-counting the 1.00x point mass; ground truth confirms
the **3% edge** (`P(crash≥X)·X ≈ 0.97` for X = 1.5 … 100).

## 2. Part A — serverSeed PRNG audit (`scripts/sh_seed_audit.py`)

The serverSeed is a 48-bit value committed before and revealed after each round.
If it were generated by a weak/predictable PRNG, the *sequence* could be predicted —
the only path to a genuine edge. It isn't:

| Test | Statistic | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Hex-digit uniformity (120,000 chars) | χ² = 15.7, p = 0.40 | ✅ uniform |
| Per-bit balance (48 bits) | 1/48 flag at p<0.01 (≈0.5 expected) | ✅ balanced |
| 48-bit integer vs Uniform | KS D = 0.005, p = 0.97 | ✅ uniform |
| Collisions in 10,000 draws | 0 (birthday exp ≈ 0) | ✅ full entropy |
| Autocorrelation (lags 1–30) | 2 exceed band (≈1.5 expected) | ✅ independent |
| Ljung–Box (10 lags) | Q = 8.75, p = 0.56 | ✅ independent |
| Lag-1 Spearman / Pearson | p = 0.49 / 0.49 | ✅ independent |
| **Time-seeding** (seed vs clock) | ρ = −0.003, p = 0.79 | ✅ clock-independent |
| **NIST SP 800-22 core (480,000 bits)** | **0 / 11 tests flag** | ✅ passes |
| LCG recovery (full-width) | modulus gcd = 1 | ✅ not an LCG |
| Linear complexity (Berlekamp–Massey) | L = 1999 ≈ n/2 | ✅ not an LFSR/xorshift |

**NIST core battery p-values** (all healthy): monobit 0.21, block-freq 0.93, runs
0.21, longest-run 0.32, matrix-rank 0.72, DFT 0.50, serial 0.21/0.09, approx-entropy
0.56, cusum 0.19/0.23. A control run on the SHA-512 `decimal` stream also passes
(0 flags), confirming the methodology.

> **A note on rigor.** On partial data the time-seeding test briefly "flagged"
> (ρ≈0.14). It was a sampling artifact: the provably-independent SHA-512 `decimal`
> **control** showed the same spurious correlation and split-half gave opposite
> signs. At n=10⁴ it vanished (ρ=−0.003). The test ships with that control built in.

**Verdict (Part A): the serverSeed behaves as a CSPRNG draw — uniform,
collision-free, serially independent, clock-independent, NIST-passing, and not a
recoverable PRNG. Future seeds cannot be predicted from past ones.**

## 3. Part B — the "hourly pattern" thesis (`scripts/sh_temporal.py`)

This is the heart of the challenge. The honest answer has three layers.

### 3.1 The observation is real — and stronger than randomness

Bucketing the 10k rounds into 51 full clock-hours and counting how often each
multiplier band appears per hour, then comparing the dispersion index
`D = Var/Mean` against a **permutation null** (shuffle which round got which
multiplier — preserves rounds/hour and the marginal distribution, destroys timing):

| Band | mean/hr | D = Var/Mean | perm 95% range | perm p | reading |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ≥2x | 92.98 | **0.282** | [0.41, 0.91] | **0.001** | more regular than chance |
| ≥5x | 37.59 | **0.442** | [0.55, 1.20] | **0.008** | more regular than chance |
| ≥10x | 18.20 | 0.578 | [0.60, 1.32] | 0.036 | borderline |
| ≥20x–≥100x | … | … | … | 0.11–0.96 | consistent w/ chance |

So per **clock-hour**, the common multipliers really do appear an unusually
*consistent* number of times — even more regular than pure chance. The permutation
test is **calibrated**: re-run on synthetic iid data with the same hour structure it
gives mean p ≈ 0.5 and flags at the nominal ~1%, so this is a *true* signal, not a
test artifact.

### 3.2 …but the round *sequence* is perfectly iid (artifact-free control)

Repeat the dispersion test on fixed-size **round** windows (no wall clock, no
hour-bucketing) and compare the Fano factor to an iid Bernoulli simulation:

| Band | window=20 | window=50 | window=190 |
|---|---|---|---|
| ≥2x | F=0.51 (iid 0.52), p=0.37 | 0.54 (0.52), p=0.64 | 0.52 (0.52), p=0.53 |
| ≥5x | 0.80 (0.81), p=0.49 | 0.86 (0.80), p=0.75 | 0.73 (0.80), p=0.36 |
| ≥10x | 0.95 (0.91), p=0.77 | 0.92 (0.90), p=0.59 | 0.95 (0.91), p=0.64 |

The under-dispersion **completely disappears**. Per *round*, the sequence is
indistinguishable from iid at every scale. So the clock-hour effect lives in the
mapping from rounds to wall-clock time — not in the outcomes themselves.

### 3.3 The mechanism: multiplier ⇒ round duration ⇒ time budget

A round's wall-clock duration is a **perfect monotone function of its multiplier**
(the curve climbs at a fixed rate, so a bigger crash simply takes longer):

| Multiplier band | mean round duration |
|---|---|
| 1.00x (instant bust) | 9.1 s |
| 1.5–2x | 14.4 s |
| 2–5x | 19.9 s |
| 5–10x | 28.2 s |
| 10–50x | 37.9 s |
| ≥50x | 57.9 s |

**Spearman(multiplier, duration) = +1.000 (p ≈ 0).** Consequently a fixed ~3600 s
clock-hour is a *time budget*: when big multipliers happen, they consume more of the
hour, so the hour mechanically holds fewer rounds and the **count of big multipliers
per clock-hour self-regulates** (under-dispersion). It is pure timing, carrying no
information about *which* round will be big.

### 3.4 Every "is it predictable?" test is null

- **Waiting-time / "due":** gaps between ≥5x/≥10x/≥20x hits fit Geometric
  (χ² p = 0.92 / 0.36 / 0.01\*) and the hazard of the next hit is **flat**
  (slopes ≈ 0, p = 0.68 / 0.33 / 0.48) — memoryless, never "overdue".
  (\*one band at 0.01 among many tests = expected noise; its hazard is flat.)
- **Time-of-day** (2.2 days): P(≥2x), P(≥10x) vs hour-of-day χ² p = 0.60 / 0.79 — none.
- **Periodicity:** FFT false-alarm p = 0.47; Lomb–Scargle peak power 0.001 — no cycle.
- **Minute-of-hour** of big multipliers: uniform (χ² p = 0.70 / 0.31) — no preferred slot.
- **Direct exploitability:** after a *hot* start within an hour, P(next ≥2x) = 0.4843
  vs 0.4828 baseline (Δ = +0.0015, p = 0.85); after *cold*, 0.4829. **Nothing to bet on.**

## 4. Part C — can active or adaptive play beat the edge? (`scripts/sh_strategy_sim.py`, `sh_daily_sim.py`, `sh_adaptive_fast.py`)

A natural objection: "I don't bet mechanically — I use money management, set daily
targets and quit while ahead, and I read trends and switch strategies." Each of
these was simulated against the 10,000 real rounds (rounds are iid, so bootstrap
resampling is valid). All three reshape the *distribution* of outcomes; none move
the *mean* above zero.

### 4.1 Money management (`sh_strategy_sim.py`)

20,000 bootstrapped sessions, start 100,000, goal 1,000,000 (10×):

| Strategy | P(reach 1M) | P(bust) | mean ROI |
|---|---|---|---|
| flat @ 2x | 0.0% | 53% | −65% |
| martingale @ 2x | 0.0% | 91% | −50% |
| **anti-martingale "let it ride" @ 2x** | **6.9%** | **93%** | **−26%** |

A 100k→1M run **is** achievable (~1 in 14 "let it ride" sessions) — so a big
winning session is real, not a fluke. But the same strategy busts 93% of the time
and averages −26%. Money management buys a better lottery ticket, not an edge.

### 4.2 "Hit a daily target, quit, repeat" (`sh_daily_sim.py`)

The optional-stopping question. Daily buy-in, play the "let it ride" style until a
daily profit target, then stop — repeated over many days:

| Plan | win-rate/day | P(net up) @100d | P(net up) @365d | mean net @365d |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| modest +20%/day | 67% | 9.2% | **0.5%** | **−3,140,000** |
| +50% / −50% daily | 34% | 12.3% | 1.5% | −2,632,000 |

You win most *days* (which is why the plan feels like it works), but **P(net up)
collapses toward zero as days accumulate** and cumulative P&L trends sharply
negative. No stopping rule beats a negative-EV game (optional stopping theorem) —
it only delays and concentrates the loss into rare catastrophic days.

### 4.3 Adaptive "read trends, ride them, switch" (`sh_adaptive_fast.py`)

The strongest version: a follow-the-leading-expert controller over a panel of
pattern experts (fixed targets + mean-reversion/"due" + momentum) that bets
whatever has worked recently and switches when it stops. The decisive test runs
the **same** strategy on the real order vs. on shuffled orders (outcomes
identical, sequence destroyed):

| | mean ROI per bet |
|---|---|
| REAL order | −3.29% |
| SHUFFLED (mean of 500) | −3.05% (±0.86%) |

**z = −0.28** — the real sequence beats only 37% of shuffles, i.e. the adaptive
strategy **cannot distinguish real game data from order-destroyed noise.** Every
expert is negative even when cherry-picked in hindsight (best: 5x target at
−2.55%); "momentum" / trend-riding was the *worst* at −3.94%. The "patterns" a
discretionary player rides are the random clustering that always exists in iid
data; they "die" because they were never predictive.

**Part C verdict:** active, staged, and adaptive play all lose at the same −3%.
Independence is a *global* property — it defeats every history-based method at once.

## 5. Why none of this is exploitable

1. **Per-round independence.** Outcomes are SHA-512 hashes of an iid serverSeed and
   the bettors' clientSeeds. History — including the hour's running tally — does not
   move the next round's probability (every conditional test = baseline).
2. **The hourly "regularity" is timing, not information.** It comes from
   multiplier→duration coupling, not from any structure you could position ahead of.
3. **No real-time leak** (prior addendum): the serverSeed is revealed only *after*
   the round, and is absent from REST and the WebSocket bus.
4. **A second barrier even in theory.** The crash hash also mixes all bettors'
   ordered `clientSeeds` (revealed post-round). Even a hypothetically predicted
   serverSeed would be insufficient to compute the outcome before betting closes.
5. **The math is unbeatable.** P(crash≥X)·X = 0.97 for every X, so every bet has the
   same −3% expected value; no selection of rounds or targets changes it.

## 6. Verdict

> **The deeper dig is done.** With 10,000 integrity-checked ground-truth rounds we
> audited the last unexamined surface — the 48-bit serverSeed generator — with a
> full NIST SP 800-22 battery, autocorrelation, time-seeding, and LCG/LFSR recovery,
> and it is **indistinguishable from a cryptographic RNG**. The "certain multipliers
> every hour" observation is **genuinely real** — counts per clock-hour are *more*
> regular than chance — but it is a **timing artifact** of multiplier-dependent round
> lengths, the underlying sequence is **iid at every scale**, and **no test makes the
> next round predictable**. There is no pattern to exploit. The only winning move
> remains not to play.
